    How to read what you see

    Five things explain almost every chart and table in the tool.

    • Bars with a vertical line through them. The bar is the actual number; the short vertical line is the benchmark (usually workforce availability, the share you'd expect). If the bar stops short of the line, the group is below the benchmark; if it pushes past, it's above.
    • Red means below, green means at or above. A red number or bar is a prompt to look closer, never a conclusion on its own.
    • Ratios. A ratio of 1.0 means a group got exactly its proportional share (of promotions, of a pay band, and so on). Below 1.0 means less than its share; above 1.0 means more.
    • "supp." means suppressed. When a count is 1 to 5 people it's hidden to protect privacy. It is not zero, it's just too small to publish.
    • Percentages are a group's share, for example "23.9% racialized" means 23.9% of those employees self-identified as a member of a visible minority.

    Three cautions to keep in mind
    1. These are screening signals, not findings. A red flag tells you where to look, not that something is wrong. Each organization has its own workforce availability reflecting its jobs and location, so a gap against the public-service average is a reason to investigate, not a verdict.
    2. The numbers can undercount. Self-identification is voluntary, so real representation may be higher than shown, and when trust is low people self-identify less, exactly where problems may be worst.
    3. Some data is linked, not embedded. Departmental promotions, acting appointments, subgroup-by-department figures and per-department survey results live in interactive federal dashboards; the tool links you straight to them.

    Key signals across the public service interpretation

    The most defensible barrier signals in the data, each linked to the section where you can verify it. These are screening signals drawn from published figures, not findings of non-compliance; the value is knowing where to look.

    1. Representation declines at the executive level for racialized employees. Racialized employees are 23.9% of the workforce and 17.3% of executives; Black employees are 5.1% of the workforce and 3.2% of executives. This descriptive gap does not, by itself, identify its cause. See Executives.
    2. Independent analysis shows a different salary distribution for Black employees. For 2024, the independent project reports a disproportionality index of 1.46 in the lowest salary range and 0.64 in the highest. Its estimated median-salary gap is independent analysis, not an official Government of Canada statistic. See Salary DI tracker.
    3. Representation declines at some recruitment stages for three of four groups. Persons with disabilities fall from 4.4% of applications to 2.4% of appointments; Black candidates from 10.3% to 6.6%. Stage-level results identify methods to review, but do not establish the cause. See Hiring & promotion.
    4. Persons with disabilities remain below the public-service-wide benchmark. Representation is 9.0% compared with 12.0% workforce availability. Some related PSES indicators also worsened. Representation itself has increased over time, so it should not be described as declining. See Groups & subgroups.
    5. Reported confidence in complaint resolution is low among affected respondents. The results raise questions about authority, reporting, reasons for not acting and satisfaction with resolution. They do not prove that an entire system has lost the trust of all employees. See Support services.
    6. The aggregate “visible minority” category conceals subgroup differences. Among employees earning $100,000 or more, published results vary substantially by subgroup. Comparisons should account for group size and other factors not shown in the table. See Salary.

    Three patterns the data already shows

    First, the executive representation gap for racialized employees: 23.9% of the workforce but 17.3% of executives, and for Black employees 5.1% of the workforce but 3.2% of executives.     Hiring, promotions and separations

    Before hiring: the recruitment funnel, stage by stage

    The PSC's Audit of Employment Equity Representation in Recruitment followed 15,285 applications to 181 externally advertised processes across 30 organizations through all five hiring stages. It is the only published dataset showing exactly where each group falls out of the funnel. The survival ratio is appointment-stage representation divided by application-stage representation: 1.00 means a group ends where it started; below 1.00 means the process itself shed them.

    The audit named the mechanism at each stage. Automated screening cost no group representation; the losses are human. Organizational screening cut racialized candidates, Black candidates hardest, with citizenship status and how experience was judged identified as factors, and visible minority candidates less successful even when they had federal experience. At assessment, written tests (mostly organization-built, open-ended, subjectively scored) hurt racialized and especially Black candidates; interviews drove the entire Indigenous drop; reference checks helped only women. The appointment stage itself cut persons with disabilities, their largest single drop. The 2023 follow-up audit on pre-assessment found the same screening also reduced representation for intersectional candidates: Indigenous people with disabilities and racialized people with disabilities. And the accountability gap: only 50% of organizations had done an employment system review in the prior 3 to 5 years, and 62% of those concluded they had no systemic barriers, while this audit was finding them. Self-assessment is not detection.

    Promotion vs acting workbench

    Acting appointments are the rehearsal space for promotion, and they are the one staffing flow missing from every TBS table. Pull your organization's numbers from the PSC subgroup dashboard (it reports employees, promotions AND acting appointments by group and subgroup per organization), enter them here, and the workbench computes both equity ratios and tells you what the pattern means. A healthy acting ratio with a weak promotion ratio, or the reverse, localizes the barrier.

    What changed, and why it matters official data

    • The benchmark for equitable representation is workforce availability (WFA), the share of the workforce, in occupations matching the public service, that belongs to each group.
    • Until recently WFA was based on the 2016 Census and counted Canadian citizens only. It is now based on the 2021 Census and counts citizens and permanent residents (Treasury Board, March 2024 reporting).
    • For racialized employees this moved the bar up by roughly six to seven points, to 22.7%. The change reflects a more recent count and a broader, fairer population, not a failure by any single department, but it does mean year-over-year representation cannot be read on its own.
    • The honest test is not "did our number go up?" but "did our gap to the current benchmark close?" The two can move in opposite directions.

    Methodology and audit trail

    The tool contains official published values, calculations made from those values, independent estimates and user-entered scenarios. Each type must be identified at the point of use.     Principles

    • Numbers are copied verbatim from official tables. Where a value was derived (for example, summing salary bands), the calculation is noted and was checked against published totals.
    • Suppressed cells are left suppressed. They are never estimated, interpolated or filled.
    • No organization's data is matched to a different organization. Where a name could not be matched across datasets, the cell is left blank.
    • Survey gaps and correlations are screening signals, not findings. They show where to look, not what is true.

    Trust and data completenessSelf-identification is voluntary, survey participation is not universal, and speaking-up and recourse measures declined on several questions.Administrative representation and survey results may understate or unevenly reflect employee experiences when participation or self-identification differs.     Recruitment-stage differencesPSC recruitment audit: overall representation did not decline at automated screening, while declines were observed for some groups at later assessment and appointment stages.     Relationship explorer: does one measure track another across organizations?

    What this is: a scatter plot. You choose a measure for the horizontal axis and another for the vertical, and each dot is one organization placed by its two values. How to read it: if the dots drift from bottom-left to top-right, the two measures tend to rise together; if top-left to bottom-right, one rises as the other falls; if they're a shapeless cloud, the two aren't related. The "r" value sums that up from -1 to +1 (0 means no link). What it's for: spotting a pattern worth a closer look, for example whether organizations with more racialized staff also have more at the executive level, and especially spotting the outliers that sit far off the trend. It is a starting question, never a conclusion.

    Salary Disproportionality Index tracker (2017–2024)

    The Disproportionality Index (DI) asks a descriptive question: within a salary range, is a group present at the same rate as all employees? A DI of 1.0 means the shares are equal. Above 1.0 means overrepresented in that range; below 1.0 means underrepresented. Concentration in lower bands and underrepresentation in higher bands may warrant further analysis, but do not by themselves show why the distribution exists. The values come from the independent Employment Equity / Disproportionality Index project, which derives them from TBS Diversity and Inclusion statistics.

    How to read this

    • The bar shows the actual result.
    • The tick or benchmark shows the comparison point.
    • A result below benchmark may signal a gap.
    • Suppressed data means the count is too small to publish. It does not mean zero.
    • Read this with related data before drawing conclusions.

    What this may mean

    A ratio below 1.0 in higher bands, or above 1.0 in lower bands, shows a salary distribution different from the overall workforce. Occupation, level, tenure, classification and career-movement data should be examined before interpreting the cause.

    About the data

    Definitions you need

    Core public administration (CPA)
    Organizations listed in schedules I and IV of the Financial Administration Act for which the Treasury Board is the employer. The current dataset contains 72 organizations. Separate agencies such as the CRA, CFIA, NRC and Parks Canada are not included and publish their own information.
    Workforce availability (WFA)
    An estimate of each designated group's share of the segments of the Canadian workforce from which the public service could recruit, using published federal methods and sources. It is used as a comparison point in employment equity analysis. It is not a ceiling, and an organization's own WFA can differ from the public-service-wide value.
    Representation
    The share of employees who voluntarily self-identified with a group. Because self-identification is voluntary, published figures may understate actual presence.
    Designated groups
    Women, Indigenous Peoples, persons with disabilities, and members of visible minorities, which is the statutory term. The site also uses “racialized employees” in plain language. Black employees are shown as a subgroup where sources publish them separately; no separate WFA is published here for that subgroup.
    Suppression
    Small cells are withheld in published material to protect confidentiality and are shown here as “supp.” A suppressed cell is not zero and must not be reconstructed.
    Promotion-share ratio
    Website calculation: a group's promotion share divided by its workforce share. Below 1.0 means its proportional share of promotions is lower than its workforce share. The ratio does not explain why.
    Salary disproportionality index
    Independent analysis: a group's share in a salary band compared with all employees' share in that band. A value above or below 1.0 describes a different concentration; it does not, by itself, establish a barrier or its cause.
    Acting appointment
    Temporarily performing the duties of a higher-level position. Access to acting appointments can be examined as a development or experience opportunity, but the data should be assessed with level, occupation, duration and staffing outcomes.

    Limits of this tool

    Key official sources and headline values were checked on July 19, 2026, including the Employment Equity Annual Report 2024-2025 (published July 7, 2026; workforce reference date March 31, 2025), its Demographic Snapshot, and TBS representation and workforce-availability series. This was not a line-by-line independent revalidation of every embedded value. Six-band salary counts were calculated by summing published detailed bands and checked against published totals. Calculated gaps, ratios, rankings, correlations, scenarios and independent median-salary estimates are identified separately. Departmental WFA, departmental promotions and acting appointments, applicant data, subgroup-by-department figures and some PSES results are available through federal dashboards and are linked rather than fully embedded. Departmental comparisons with public-service-wide WFA are screening signals; organization-specific WFA can differ because occupational and geographic composition differs. TBS reporting also notes that differences between workforce-availability and self-identification definitions can affect comparisons for persons with disabilities. Separations include retirements. Legal and policy references are informational summaries and should be checked against the current official instrument before use.

    Definitions in plain language

    The terms used in this tool, in plain language. The technical term is shown in parentheses where it differs.

    Employment equity
    A legal duty to identify and remove barriers so the workforce reflects the people available to do the work, for the four designated groups.
    Designated group
    The four groups named in the Employment Equity Act: women, Indigenous Peoples, persons with disabilities, and members of visible minorities (racialized employees).
    Subgroup
    A more specific group within a designated group, such as First Nations, Métis or Inuit within Indigenous Peoples, or Black employees within racialized employees.
    Workforce availability (WFA)
    An estimate of a group's share of the segments of the Canadian workforce from which the organization could recruit, using published federal sources and methods. It is used as a comparison point in employment equity analysis; it is neither a ceiling nor necessarily each organization's own WFA.
    Benchmark
    The comparison point a result is measured against. Here it is usually workforce availability.
    Representation
    The share of employees who have voluntarily self-identified with a group. Because it is voluntary, the true number may be higher.
    Representation gap
    Representation minus the benchmark, in percentage points. A negative gap means the group is below the level the available workforce would predict.
    Suppression
    When a count is too small to publish (often 1 to 5 people), it is withheld to protect privacy. A suppressed cell is not zero.
    Ratio
    One share divided by another. Below 1.0 means less than expected; above 1.0 means more than expected.
    Disproportionality index
    A group's share within a salary band compared with its share of the whole workforce. Above 1.0 means over-represented in that band; below 1.0, under-represented.
    Core public administration (CPA)
    The roughly 72 organizations where the Treasury Board is the employer. Separate agencies such as the CRA or Parks Canada report on their own.
    Public service
    The broader federal public service, including separate agencies. Wider than the core public administration.
    Department
    A federal organization. Here, an employer covered by the data, used interchangeably with organization.
    Public Service Employee Survey (PSES)
    A confidential survey of federal employees on their workplace experience, run every one to two years.
    Hiring
    Bringing a person into a job from outside or through a staffing process, measured here at stages from application to appointment.
    Promotion
    Moving to a higher-level position. A main path for career progression.
    Executive (EX)
    The senior leadership group (EX classifications), below the deputy head level.
    EX feeder group
    The levels just below executive (often EX minus 1 and EX minus 2) from which most executives are drawn.
    Self-identification
    Voluntarily telling the employer that you belong to a designated group. It is how representation is counted.
    Disaggregated data
    Data broken down into smaller categories, such as by group, subgroup, level, branch or region, rather than reported as one total.

    Limitations of the data

    What this public data can and cannot show. Read this before drawing conclusions.

    A gap is a signal to investigate, not a final finding. This tool uses language such as "may signal", "may suggest", "should be investigated", and "cannot be concluded from this data alone". It does not state that discrimination has been proven.

    What this data does not show

    • Public datasets may not show process-level barriers (who decided what, and how).
    • Suppressed values do not mean zero; they mean the count was too small to publish.
    • Self-identification is voluntary, so representation may understate the true numbers.
    • A department's own workforce availability may differ from the public-service-wide benchmark, reflecting its occupations and region.
    • Data may not include branch, region, classification, occupational group, language profile, security level, or process stage.
    • Some findings require additional departmental data before conclusions can be drawn.
