Getting around

  • Tabs across the top are the sections. On a phone they wrap onto more than one row; scroll up to see them all. You can also move between them with the arrow keys.
  • Switching scope. Most pages let you choose the whole public service, a single department, or a specific group, using the dropdowns and buttons near the top of each page.
  • Getting data out. Every table has a Copy button (pastes into Excel) and a CSV button (downloads the table). Every section has a Print / Save as PDF button for briefing notes.

How to read what you see

Five things explain almost every chart and table in the tool.

  • Bars with a vertical line through them. The bar is the actual number; the short vertical line is the benchmark (usually workforce availability, the share you'd expect). If the bar stops short of the line, the group is below the benchmark; if it pushes past, it's above.
  • Red means below, green means at or above. A red number or bar is a prompt to look closer, never a conclusion on its own.
  • Ratios. A ratio of 1.0 means a group got exactly its proportional share (of promotions, of a pay band, and so on). Below 1.0 means less than its share; above 1.0 means more.
  • "supp." means suppressed. When a count is 1 to 5 people it's hidden to protect privacy. It is not zero, it's just too small to publish.
  • Percentages are a group's share, for example "23.9% racialized" means 23.9% of those employees self-identified as a member of a visible minority.

Three cautions to keep in mind

  1. These are screening signals, not findings. A red flag tells you where to look, not that something is wrong. Each organization has its own workforce availability reflecting its jobs and location, so a gap against the public-service average is a reason to investigate, not a verdict.
  2. The numbers can undercount. Self-identification is voluntary, so real representation may be higher than shown, and when trust is low people self-identify less, exactly where problems may be worst.
  3. Some data is linked, not embedded. Departmental promotions, acting appointments, subgroup-by-department figures and per-department survey results live in interactive federal dashboards; the tool links you straight to them.

Three patterns the data already shows

First, the executive cliff for racialized employees: 23.9% of the workforce but 17.3% of executives, and for Black employees 5.1% of the workforce but 3.2% of executives. Second, salary compression for Black employees: 40.8% earn between $50,000 and $74,999 compared with 27.9% of all employees, the pattern the Disproportionality Index study first documented. Third, the disability availability gap: representation rose to 9.0% but remains 3 points below the 12.0% availability estimate; part of that distance reflects the newer, broader survey definition of disability that the self-identification form does not yet use. Each pattern has its own section in this tool.

Salary and pay equity

Who sits where on the pay scale. The disproportionality ratio compares each group's share in a salary band with the share of all employees in that band: above 1.0 means overrepresented in the band, below 1.0 means underrepresented. Overrepresentation in low bands plus underrepresentation in high bands is the compression pattern that signals blocked progression.

Before hiring: the recruitment funnel, stage by stage

The PSC's Audit of Employment Equity Representation in Recruitment followed 15,285 applications to 181 externally advertised processes across 30 organizations through all five hiring stages. It is the only published dataset showing exactly where each group falls out of the funnel. The survival ratio is appointment-stage representation divided by application-stage representation: 1.00 means a group ends where it started; below 1.00 means the process itself shed them.

The audit named the mechanism at each stage. Automated screening cost no group representation; the losses are human. Organizational screening cut racialized candidates, Black candidates hardest, with citizenship status and how experience was judged identified as factors, and visible minority candidates less successful even when they had federal experience. At assessment, written tests (mostly organization-built, open-ended, subjectively scored) hurt racialized and especially Black candidates; interviews drove the entire Indigenous drop; reference checks helped only women. The appointment stage itself cut persons with disabilities, their largest single drop. The 2023 follow-up audit on pre-assessment found the same screening also reduced representation for intersectional candidates: Indigenous people with disabilities and racialized people with disabilities. And the accountability gap: only 50% of organizations had done an employment system review in the prior 3 to 5 years, and 62% of those concluded they had no systemic barriers, while this audit was finding them. Self-assessment is not detection.

Employee support services and outcomes

Funded employee networks, an ombuds office, an informal conflict management system, an anti-racism or IDEA team: these are the structures organizations use to surface and resolve workplace problems. This page makes the data case for them and lets you compare organizations by the services they offer. The argument is usually made with values; here it is made with numbers, the 2024 survey shows, in the public service's own words, where the formal system loses people, which is the gap these services exist to close.

What these services are

An ombuds office is an independent, impartial, confidential and informal channel for raising and resolving concerns outside the chain of command. Funded employee networks give equity-seeking staff a collective voice and a route to leadership. An informal conflict management system (ICMS) is required of every deputy head under section 207 of the Federal Public Sector Labour Relations Act. An anti-racism or IDEA team or secretariat carries dedicated capacity for systemic change. Many of the survey questions below speak to all of them, not the ombuds alone.

The statutory anchor: section 207 of the Federal Public Sector Labour Relations Act requires every deputy head to establish an informal conflict management system in consultation with bargaining agents; an ombuds office is the most developed form of that obligation. The pattern data these services aggregate also feeds the Employment Equity Act section 5 duty to identify and eliminate barriers, and hazard prevention obligations under Canada Labour Code Part II.

Build the financial case: an interactive calculator you enter

Starting values are the official benchmarks from the evaluations above (manager time lost to conflict, replacement cost at 150% of compensation). The default cost per employee, $125,000, is the Parliamentary Budget Officer's average personnel cost per full-time equivalent (salary plus standard compensation; the PBO reports total federal personnel spending of $71.1 billion in 2024-25). Average base salary across the service is roughly $100,000 (Treasury Board). Replace any value with your own organization's figures. The model uses two conservative, direct mechanisms: time recovered from unresolved conflict, and the cost differential when matters resolve informally instead of formally. Indirect effects (turnover avoided, sick leave, legal exposure) are deliberately excluded so the result survives scrutiny.

Every assumption is visible and editable, which is the point: bring your organization's grievance counts, your ICMS intake data and your own salary mix, and the model becomes evidence rather than estimate.

Real progress, or just optics?

Representation can rise while the gap to the benchmark widens, because the benchmark itself moved. In 2023 the workforce-availability benchmark was updated to the 2021 Census and, for the first time, counts both Canadian citizens and permanent residents. For racialized (visible minority) employees that raised the bar substantially. A department whose representation held steady can now sit further behind, and one that posted a gain can still have lost ground against the higher benchmark. This page shows the full picture so a moved goalpost is not mistaken for a win.

What changed, and why it matters official data

  • The benchmark for equitable representation is workforce availability (WFA), the share of the workforce, in occupations matching the public service, that belongs to each group.
  • Until recently WFA was based on the 2016 Census and counted Canadian citizens only. It is now based on the 2021 Census and counts citizens and permanent residents (Treasury Board, March 2024 reporting).
  • For racialized employees this moved the bar up by roughly six to seven points, to 22.7%. The change reflects a more recent count and a broader, fairer population, not a failure by any single department, but it does mean year-over-year representation cannot be read on its own.
  • The honest test is not "did our number go up?" but "did our gap to the current benchmark close?" The two can move in opposite directions.

Racialized representation against the current (raised) benchmark

Each organization's racialized representation against the 22.7% public-service workforce-availability benchmark. Red sits below it. Many organizations that look healthy on a raw representation number are below the benchmark once the bar is set where it now stands. Sort and filter to see who is genuinely at parity.

How to talk about it honestly

If your organization's racialized representation rose but is still below 22.7%, the accurate statement is that representation improved and a gap to workforce availability remains. If it rose and the benchmark rose faster, say so. Claiming parity because a raw number climbed, when the gap to the current benchmark persists, is the optics trap this page exists to prevent. The same caution applies in reverse: a department that looks worse against the new benchmark has not necessarily regressed; the bar moved.

Relationship explorer: does one measure track another across organizations?

What this is: a scatter plot. You choose a measure for the horizontal axis and another for the vertical, and each dot is one organization placed by its two values. How to read it: if the dots drift from bottom-left to top-right, the two measures tend to rise together; if top-left to bottom-right, one rises as the other falls; if they're a shapeless cloud, the two aren't related. The "r" value sums that up from -1 to +1 (0 means no link). What it's for: spotting a pattern worth a closer look, for example whether organizations with more racialized staff also have more at the executive level, and especially spotting the outliers that sit far off the trend. It is a starting question, never a conclusion.

Salary Disproportionality Index tracker (2017–2024)

The Disproportionality Index (DI) asks a simple question: within a given salary range, is a group present at the rate you would expect? A DI of 1.0 means a group sits in that salary range at exactly the same rate as all employees. Above 1.0 means overrepresented in that range; below 1.0 means underrepresented. The telltale sign of blocked pay progression is a group stacking up above 1.0 in the lowest ranges and dropping below 1.0 in the highest. These are the published figures from the independent Employment Equity / Disproportionality Index project, which derives them from TBS Diversity and Inclusion statistics.

DI for a group in a salary range = (that group's % in the range) ÷ (all employees' % in the same range). Example from the source: in 2024, 34.78% of Black employees but 23.87% of all employees earned in the lowest range, giving a DI of 34.78 ÷ 23.87 = 1.46. Salary ranges shift year to year because they track the median; the source re-bins each year, which is why the band labels below change with the year you pick.

About the data

Definitions you need

Core public administration (CPA)
The 72 organizations where Treasury Board is the employer. Separate agencies such as CRA, CFIA, NRC and Parks Canada have their own reports.
Workforce availability (WFA)
The estimated share of each designated group in the Canadian workforce who could be recruited into public service jobs, based on the 2021 Census and 2022 Canadian Survey on Disability. It is the legal benchmark under the Employment Equity Act, and TBS itself calls it a starting point, not a ceiling.
Representation
The share of employees who have voluntarily self-identified with a group. Because self-identification is voluntary, true numbers may be higher than reported.
Designated groups
Women, Indigenous Peoples, persons with disabilities, and members of visible minorities (racialized employees), as set in the Employment Equity Act. Black employees are reported separately as a subgroup since 2022 in response to the Clerk's Call to Action; there is no WFA for Black employees because the Act does not designate the subgroup.
Suppression
Counts of 1 to 5 people are hidden in published tables to protect privacy, shown here as "supp." A suppressed cell is not zero.
Promotion equity ratio
A group's share of promotions divided by its share of the workforce. Below 1.0 means the group receives fewer promotions than its size predicts.
Disproportionality ratio
A group's share in a salary band divided by all employees' share in the same band. Above 1.0 in low bands and below 1.0 in high bands signals blocked pay progression.
Acting appointment
Temporarily performing a higher-level job. Acting experience is a common gateway to promotion, which makes equitable access to acting a leading indicator.

Honest limits of this tool

Every number here was transcribed on June 11, 2026 from tables published by the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat in the Employment Equity Annual Report 2024-25 and its Demographic Snapshot, plus the TBS representation and WFA series. Six-band salary counts were produced by summing TBS's published detailed bands and verified against published totals; nothing is estimated, interpolated or modelled. Departmental WFA, departmental promotions and acting appointments, applicant data, subgroup-by-department figures and all PSES results exist only inside interactive federal dashboards and are linked, not copied. Departments below the public-service-wide availability line are screening signals: each department has its own WFA reflecting its occupation and region mix, available inside the PSC dashboard. The disability WFA of 12.0% uses the expanded 2017+ survey definition that the current self-identification form does not, so part of that gap reflects measurement, and TBS expects the modernized self-ID questionnaire to close much of it. Separations include retirements. Legal anchors for action: Employment Equity Act s.5 (duty to identify and eliminate barriers), Canadian Human Rights Act, the Clerk's Call to Action, and the CHRC's 2024 horizontal audit of racialized employment.
